Section 1: The Road South

By the eighth day south of Freehaven, the roads had surrendered any claim to the name. They were muddy arguments between wheels, hooves, and spring rain.

The delegation stretched along the forest margin in a line of damp cloaks, packhorses, and complaints. Fifty souls all told: light for a royal mission, heavy enough to churn the track into mire. Two wagons carried gifts, ledgers, tack, and the comforts courtiers believed necessary to represent a kingdom properly.

At the head rode Riven, king of Freehaven, his dark green cloak darker still with rain. He had ridden most of the morning in silence, not from aloofness, but attention. His eyes moved constantly over tree line, water, weather, horses, and road, with the old habit of a man measuring the world for danger and passage.

He reined in where the road dipped through black standing water.

“Off the center,” he called. “Left shoulder. Single file. Light wagon first.”

The order passed back. The first wagon turned, held for three breaths, then dropped to the axle with a wet, sucking sound. The team panicked.
|
Riven was in the mud before the driver could climb down. He caught the nearest horse by the bridle, his voice low and steady speaking in elven. “Easy. Easy now.”

Then came the work. “Unhitch the left trace. Cut brush there. Chocks from the baggage cart. Joran, bring the spare line.”

There was no royal reluctance in him. When the time came, he set his shoulder to the wheel with the others, boots sinking deep, rain running from his hair. At his count, the wagon lurched, stuck, lurched again, then rose in a spray of muck and rolled free.

A cheer went up from the younger guards. One courtier stared at Riven equal parts astonished and appalled.

Riven looked once at the clouds. “We lose another half hour if we stay low. We’ll take the deer path on the rise and cut east around the marsh.”

The envoy beside him blinked. “There is a path there?”

“There will be.” 
By noon they had left the drowned road behind and were threading through open timber on a track that existed mostly to Riven’s eyes: bent grass, broken fern, old sign. 

The wagons scraped past alder and fir; the soldiers moved easier; even the horses seemed relieved to be on honest ground.
There was still grumbling. A courtier objected to branches. Another to cold provisions. A sergeant, already short of patience, observed that leaves were unlikely to kill anyone.

Riven let his horse drift back between them.

“Problem?”

“The dignity of the crown,” the courtier said stiffly, “is not well served by this treatment.”

Riven glanced at the mud, the trees, the wagons, the weary horses. “The dignity of the crown will survive lunch under trees.”

Linzi rode up beside him once the matter had settled.

She studied him. “When you said we would travel swiftly, I imagined urgency. Not an organized campaign against comfort.”

“Comfort slows people.”

“So does exhaustion.”

“Less permanently.”

“This is why courts prefer softer rulers. They are less educational.”

He adjusted his reins. “And yet you came.”

“Of course I came. Left alone, you would establish trade with three nations, offend two border lords, sleep in the rain by preference, and arrive in Kyonin claiming surprise at your own feelings.”

His expression did not change, which on Riven was often suspicious. “I don’t know what you mean.”

“No? Then I imagined the way you look south every time the road opens?”

For a few moments there was only rain dripping from branches and hooves on damp earth.

“The road is south,” he said.

“Mm.”

“You are insufferable.”

“And indispensable. A burden you bear nobly.”
That drew the faintest smile from him, gone almost before it appeared.

Toward evening the rain cleared. Riven called a halt on a low rise above a narrow stream: water close, ground dry, room for wagons, cover from wind, three clean approaches, trees thick enough to screen them but not hide an enemy within bowshot.
He assigned watches, placed pickets, checked the horse lines, and walked the ground himself before the first pot was hung. What had begun as a diplomatic procession settled into something safer and truer: a company under the care of a man who knew how to move others through uncertain country.

Later, Linzi found Riven beyond the firelight, one hand resting on a low branch, looking south through the trees.

“It was a good camp,” she said.

He nodded. “Ground’s sound. We can make distance tomorrow.”

She looked at him in profile. “You are happy out here.”

Riven gazed into the darkening wood.

“Yes,” he said.

Linzi followed his gaze.

“Well,” she said softly, “do try not to look so heroic every time you stare into the middle distance. It alarms the courtiers.”

Riven glanced at her, and this time the smile stayed.

“I’ll do my best.”

“Good. Because tomorrow I intend to complain about the pace again, and I prefer an audience not entirely overcome by admiration.”

She left him then, returning to the fire where voices rose with the sparks. Riven remained beneath the trees a little longer, looking south, the old road and the new one running together at last.

Section 2: The Embeth Forest 
Wes [Riven] — 12:49 PM
They entered Embeth beneath tall pines and a hard gray sky. The forest closed around them, quieter than the Stolen Lands and far older in its silences.

A whistle cut through the trees.

The Freehaven riders checked, hands moving toward weapons. Riven lifted one hand without looking back.

Figures stepped from the forest in mottled greens and browns, bows unstrung but near. At their center walked a broad-shouldered man in a weather-dark cloak, beard shorter than Riven remembered, face more lined, eyes just as sharp.

Jameel Visser.

He looked Riven over: the column, the envoys, the soldiers, the bow at his back. Then his mouth bent into a grin.

“Well,” Jameel said, “good to see the crown hasn’t made you soft.”

Linzi made a small, delighted sound.

Riven swung down from his horse. “You look older.”

“I am older. You look tired.”

“I am tired.”

Jameel laughed once and clasped forearms with him, brisk and hard. “Not slower. My scouts found your trail at dawn and were still cursing your pace an hour later.”

“One should keep scouts humble.”
“I remember an elf scout who found humility very optional.”

The foresters laughed. Some of Riven’s escort relaxed. The courtiers looked uncertain whether this was welcome or insult.

Linzi rode up and tipped her head. “I am delighted to see His Majesty received with the reverence he merits.”

Jameel looked at her, amused. “And you are?”

“Linzi of Freehaven. Councilor, foreign minister, and the only thing standing between our king and several preventable diplomatic incidents.”

“Then you have my sympathy.”

“My gratitude,” Linzi said. “Sympathy I reserve for those still learning his notion of a light march.”

This time Jameel laughed properly.
They were led to a ranger hall tucked between two rises beside a cold stream. It had no banners, no carved proclamation of importance. The paths were swept, the roof was sound, the woodpile was dry, and no one would find it by accident.

Inside, they were given venison stew, black bread, dried apples, and hot broth for riders damp from the road. Riven took his place without ceremony. Linzi moved among the introductions with a smile, a question, a remembered name, turning wary pauses into conversation before anyone noticed she had done it.

Jameel did not speak to Riven as though to a king in borrowed boots. He spoke to him as one forester to another.

“So,” he said at last, cup in hand. “It’s true? You made a kingdom of that rough country north?”

“We did.”

“Bandits gone. Roads cut. New towns?”

“All true.”

“And Pitax?”

Riven’s face did not change. “Irovetti is dead. Pitax is part of Freehaven now.”

A murmur moved around the table.

Jameel studied him. “You always were dangerous,” he said. “But this…” He gestured, not quite at Riven nor at the company. “This I did not expect.”

Riven looked down at his cup. “Neither did I.”

That earned a few smiles.

After the meal, the maps came out: trade paths, fordable crossings, monster sign, old hunting roads marked in charcoal. Riven stood beside Jameel and the senior wardens, Linzi at his left with her notes already open.
They spoke of caravans, tolls, raiders, guides, warnings, and border custom. When the talk turned to roads, patrols, or wild country, Riven answered plainly. When it turned to tariffs, recognition, and embassies, Linzi entered with such light precision that one of Jameel’s captains began watching her the way men watched drawn steel.

Then an older warden with ash-gray braids said, “Freehaven is young. Young realms break.”

Riven met his gaze. “Old ones break too.”

The table quieted.
“We are young,” Riven said. “We were born in hard country. We have held against bandits, monsters, famine, plague, cults, and war. Not because we were spared. Because we held together.”

No one answered at once.

Jameel looked around the table, then grunted. “Aye. Sounds like a kingdom.”

Linzi, without looking up from her notes, said, “Do remember you said that. I shall likely want it written down.”

“There’s the minister speaking.”

“And the chronicler,” she said. “I am very efficient.”

By dusk, nothing had been signed, but something had shifted. Safe conduct for Freehaven caravans. Open communication between ranger companies. Shared warning of threats moving between territories. Not alliance yet, but recognition. Freehaven would not be treated as a camp that had grown too large for its own good.

When the hall quieted and the maps were rolled away, Jameel stepped outside with Riven beneath the pines. Rainwater fell slowly from the branches. Pale gold light lingered between the trunks.

“You’ve changed,” Jameel said.

Riven looked into the trees. “Yes.”

“Not how I expected. Thought you’d grow harder if you lived long enough. Meaner, maybe.”

Riven was quiet a moment. “I tried that.”

“Aye,” Jameel said. “You did.” He frowned, annoyed by the need to find the right words. 

“Now you look like you know what you’re for.”

Riven did not answer. The words had found their mark.

From the doorway, Linzi called, “If you are done saying rugged and emotionally significant things under the trees, Your Majesty, your supper is cooling. I have spent the better part of an hour convincing these good people Freehaven is governed by civilized beings.”

Jameel laughed outright.

Riven glanced back. “And have you succeeded?”

“Barely. Do not undo it.”

Jameel shook his head. “Keep her close, Riven.”

“I intend to.”
“Good. She makes you almost approachable.”

Linzi came down the steps, smiling as though she had heard enough to be pleased and not enough to become sentimental. Together they turned back toward the hall.

Later that night, Riven stood outside before sleep, listening to the stream and the low voices of wardens changing watch. Beyond Embeth lay older woods, older roads, and somewhere ahead, a golden-haired woman he had not yet found words enough to face.

Linzi appeared at his elbow.

“No,” she said.

Riven looked at her. “No what?”

“No practicing.”

“I wasn’t.”

“You were. It has the look of tragedy about it. Don’t do that. You’ll frighten her.”

Despite himself, he huffed a laugh.

Linzi folded her arms. “You did well today. You impressed veteran rangers, secured goodwill, and behaved almost flawlessly. Let that be enough for one evening.”

“And tomorrow?”

“Tomorrow,” she said, turning back toward the warm light of the hall, “you may begin the much harder work of not making a complete fool of yourself in Kyonin.”

She left him with that, and because she was Linzi, it steadied him better than comfort would have.

Section 3: At the Border of Kyonin
Wes [Riven] — 1:10 PM
They crossed into Kyonin at dawn.

There was no wall, no gate, no cut in the earth to say where one realm ended and another began. The change came in the trees first: the lift of the branches, the green light gathered under them, the scent of water over stone. He knew these woods.

Riven reined in before he meant to.
Behind him, the delegation slowed in a soft confusion of tack and hooves. No one spoke. Even the horses lowered their heads as if listening.

Ahead, beeches and silver-barked alders made a long green passage. Dew brightened the ferns. Somewhere out of sight, a thrush sang once, then again, and the sound reached into him so cleanly that for a moment he could not tell whether it hurt or healed.

Linzi came up on his left. She said nothing. She had learned when silence was doing honest work.

Somewhere beyond these ridges lay Avennara. Beyond streams, old roads, and glades he had not seen in thirty years stood the places where he had trained, hunted, failed, and left too quickly.

A young courtier whispered, “Gods.”

Linzi looked into the trees. “Yes. Someone took great pains with it.”

A faint laugh moved through the line, just enough to let everyone breathe again.

Riven touched heel to horse and rode on.
The road through Kyonin did not conquer the land. It bent with it, following ridges and streams, vanishing now and then beneath leaves before appearing again between white stones etched with weather-softened script. They crossed a pale bridge over a narrow gorge, its rail half-veiled in ivy. They passed a ruined shrine ringed with lilies and foxglove, kept not by hands but by the care of the place itself.

The delegation rode quietly. Even the soldiers seemed to understand that here, noise was a kind of clumsiness.
Riven answered when asked in the lyrical elven tongue. He named a tree, an old border path, a stream that ran south after spring rains. His voice remained even, but Linzi saw how his eyes paused at certain bends, certain stones, certain hollows filled with fern. It was not quite recognition. It was memory finding him.

By midday they halted beside a spring. Horses drank. Cloaks were spread to dry. Cold provisions were unpacked with unusual care, as though the forest might object to crumbs.

Riven walked twenty paces from the clearing and stood among the beeches, one hand resting against a smooth white trunk. Light moved over him in green and gold. Nearby, water slipped over stone.

Linzi found him there with two cups of watered wine.

“You vanished with suspicious speed,” she said, offering one.

“I walked twenty paces.”

“For you, that is nearly an abdication.”

He took the cup. “Thank you.”

She leaned against a neighboring tree. “You have been quieter since dawn.”

“It has been a long time.”

“Yes.”

He looked through the trees. “I had forgotten some of it.”

“No,” Linzi said. “I don’t think you had.”

He went still.

From the clearing came the clatter of a dropped kettle, followed by an apology. Neither of them turned.

At last Riven said, “It feels smaller than I remembered.”

“That is either wisdom or height.”

His mouth shifted.

“Not lesser,” he said. “Just…” He looked past her, into the layered green. “Nearer.”

Linzi nodded. “That sounds more like it.”

For a while they stood with the spring talking over the stones.

Then Linzi said, “You are deciding whether to tell me, or whether I will have to drag it out of you.”

Riven glanced at her. “Tell you what?”

She gave him a look of patient injury. “You can read a broken fern at thirty paces, but plain conversation remains beyond you.”

He drank his wine.

“The mission matters,” she said. “Freehaven matters. But that is not all that brought you here.”
The leaves moved above them. Light shifted along the white bark beneath his hand.

There had been a time when he would have denied it, not to deceive her, but because duty was easier to carry than want. He had worn that habit for years. It no longer fit him as well.

“No,” he said. “It isn’t.”

Linzi waited.

“I mean to find Lyriel.”

She did not smile in triumph. She only inclined her head. “Yes.”

That gentleness undid him more than surprise would have.

“I wrote to her before the tournament,” he said.

“I know.”

“She did not come.”

“No.”

There was no pity in it.

Riven looked toward the spring. “I had thought…” He stopped, jaw tightening. “No. I had hoped.”

Linzi waited.

“I won the archery contest,” he said. “I thought perhaps word would reach her. Perhaps she would come and see…” He gave a faint shake of his head. “Then Pitax came, and there was no room for any of it.”

“No,” Linzi said. “There would not have been.”

He turned the cup in his hands. “I do not know whether she will want to see me.”

Linzi watched him for a moment. “Do you want her forgiveness, or her company?”

He frowned, not at her, but at the question. 

“Both.”

“That is honest,” she said. “But they are not the same thing.”

He looked at her then.

The humor had not left Linzi’s face, but something steadier stood behind it. “You can ask to speak with her. You can tell her the truth. You can tell her who you were, who you are, and what you hope for now. But you cannot arrive with a crown and a record of victories and expect her heart to salute.”
He stared at Linzi.

“I am serious.”

“I know.”

“She is not the last line of an old song waiting for you to remember it. She is a woman who was hurt, and who has lived twenty years without arranging herself around your return. Go to her as yourself. Not as a king presenting proof. Not as a penitent trying to make suffering useful. Just yourself.”

Riven looked down into the cup. Pale light trembled on the wine.

“For a long time,” he said, “I thought I had to return worthy. Perhaps remade.”

“And now?”

The sounds of the clearing drifted close again: a horse stamping, low voices, the kettle set right at last.

“Now,” he said, “I think I have to return honestly.”

Linzi smiled then, warm and small. “There. Much worse. Much better.”

He shook his head. “You always make it sound simple.”

“No. I make it sound possible. Those are different.”

They began walking back toward the clearing.

After a few paces, Linzi glanced up at him. “Also, for the sake of Freehaven, I must insist on one thing.”

Riven eyed her. “What?”

“If, when you see her, you say anything that sounds as though it belongs carved over a doorway, I shall throw a pinecone at you.”

He stared at her.

“I mean it.”

“You think so little of me?”

“I think exactly enough of you. You are very good at truth when cornered and very bad at it when given time to prepare.”

“That may be fair.”

“It is entirely fair. If you begin with, ‘Lyriel, fairest star of my…’”

“I would not.”
“Good. Because then I should have to intervene, and where would we be? Kyonin offended, diplomacy ruined, romance in ashes.”

“At least your priorities are clear.”

“They always are.”

By the time they reached the clearing, some of the strain had gone from him. Not all of it. Enough that it no longer held him by the throat.

The delegation was preparing to move. Harness was tightened. Packs were fastened. An envoy came forward with a question about the next stop, and Riven answered cleanly. The work of the road gathered around him again, immediate and useful.

He mounted and turned his horse south.

Before giving the signal, he looked once more into the green depth of Kyonin: the country of his birth, his shame, his first love, and the self he had once tried to leave behind. The white stones marked the way ahead. Leaves moved in the morning light.

Then he rode on.

Behind him, Linzi settled into her saddle and sighed with theatrical patience.

“Well,” she murmured to her horse, “he has admitted it. That is nearly miraculous. Now I need only prevent him from saying something magnificent and foolish when the time comes.”

The horse gave no opinion.

Linzi accepted this as agreement, and the company passed beneath the bright leaves, deeper into Kyonin.

Section 4: The Amulet
The house set aside for Freehaven’s delegation stood at the edge of the district beneath ancient boughs, its carved galleries open to the night. Beyond the lantern light, silver leaves stirred in the dark. Somewhere below, water moved with the same clear voice it had possessed when Riven was young.

The hour was late. The household had gone quiet. Even the sentries below seemed to keep their stillness in deference to the place.

Riven stood alone on the upper veranda, cloak unfastened, the cool air against his face. He could see little beyond the pale tracery of branches and the faint catch of starlight in leaves, but he knew these woods by older senses: scent, breath, the weight of silence.

Kyonin had not waited for him. That was plain enough.

And still, it remained.

He rested one hand on the railing and listened. He had crossed mountains, marshes, tombs, and battlefields. He had stood in halls where men screamed and on ramparts under arrowfall. Yet this quiet elven night unsettled him more than any of them.

After a long while, he reached beneath his tunic.

The amulet came free into his hand on its worn cord, light as ever, though it had traveled with him for twenty years.

Lyriel had given it to him the day he left Kyonin, when leaving had seemed to him the last dignity left to a ruined man. He remembered her hands trembling once, then going still. He remembered her eyes. Not disbelieving. Worse than that. Understanding enough to know he had already gone somewhere she could not follow.
He had promised to return. Even then, he had known the promise was made of pain, not certainty. He had said it because silence would have been unbearable.

For years he had made that parting part of the story of his disgrace. The village lost. His rank stripped from him. His honor broken in public. Lyriel, too, became one more thing his failure had taken.

It was not false.

It was only smaller than the truth.
Riven turned the amulet over, thumb finding the familiar worn edge.

He had not left only because of shame before his commanders, or the council, or the soldiers who had watched his insignia taken from him. Their judgment had cut deeply, but cleanly. He understood that kind of pain.

What he could not bear was Lyriel seeing him ruined and loving him still.

He had told himself he was sparing her. A kinder lie than most, and therefore more dangerous. In truth, he had denied her the right to choose. He had made his shame lord over them both and called it honor.

The leaves stirred overhead.

Somewhere in the dark, a night bird called once and fell silent.

Riven closed his hand around the amulet and bowed his head. Not as penance. He had done enough of that, and badly. This was acknowledgment.

He had loved her. That had always been real. She had been the truest thing in his life before it broke. But love had not made him brave where it mattered. In that hour, he had chosen absence over intimacy. Distance over trust. Fine words had come after: duty, honor, necessity. The plain words had waited twenty years.

I was ashamed.

I could not stay.

I did not know how to be loved before I felt worthy of it.

He opened his hand again. The amulet lay against his palm, silvered by starlight.

The knowledge no longer struck like a blade. It settled with the steadier weight of something finally named.
He had done real harm. Not the loud harm of betrayal or cruelty, but the quieter kind: years unanswered, a promise left to weather, a grief forced to make its own shelter.
And yet he was not the young captain who had walked away.

That, too, had taken years to learn.

The road from Kyonin had not redeemed him by some clean exchange of suffering for worth. It had led through hunger, anger, poor loyalties, blood, and long wandering without shape. Then, by degrees, it had led him to work and purpose.

A trading post harried by bandits. Roads that needed cutting. People who needed more than a sword drawn in their defense. A fort taken in darkness and made into a town. A town made into a kingdom. Hard decisions. Hard peace. Friends who did not let him disappear into himself. A people who needed him present, not perfect.
He loved Freehaven. Not because it excused his past. Because there, at last, he had stopped living only in answer to it.

That mattered.

It meant he had not come back to Kyonin to beg the past to bless him. He had not come to lay a crown over an old wound and call it healed. He had come as the elf he was: marked, answerable, imperfect, and no longer hollowed out by the need to prove his life deserved to continue.

Tomorrow, or the day after, he would go to Lyriel.

He would not bring her speeches. He would not dress absence as fate, or ask war and kingship to stand witness on his behalf. He would not ask her to look only at what he had become and not at what he had done.
He would tell her the truth.

That he had loved her.

That he loved her still.

That shame had mastered him.

That he had mistaken self-punishment for honor.

That whatever worth he had now lay not in being blameless, but in standing before her without pride for armor.
The night pressed cool against his skin. Above him, the stars shone through the high branches in broken fragments.

Riven slipped the amulet back beneath his tunic, close against his heart.
For a while longer he remained on the veranda. Sorrow was still there. Tenderness too. The old ache had not vanished; it had only changed its name. It was no longer shame alone. It was love, carried too long in silence.
When he finally turned and went inside, he did not feel absolved. He did not feel certain.
But he was no longer asking the dark to make him someone else before morning.

Section 5: Reunion in the Garden
Wes [Riven] — 2:05 PM
Lyriel’s house stood where the city loosened into old gardens, and the gardens into wood.

It was not grand. Pale stone showed through climbing green. Narrow windows stood open to the afternoon. The paths had been swept, but not tamed; early flowers leaned where they pleased, and bees moved through the light with the drowsy certainty of creatures long welcome there. A little fountain ran beneath the trees.

He stood at the gate resting his hand there for too long. After a few breaths, he opened it.

The path curved through lavender and white flowering shrubs toward the rear of the house. He saw the easel first, set near a stand of pale-barked trees. Beside it stood a small table: brushes in a careful row, cloths folded and used again, a cup of rinse water clouded blue-green, dishes of paint catching the light.

Then Lyriel.

She stood with one hand braced lightly against the easel, brush lifted, head turned as she considered the canvas. She wore a simple gown, cream with green at the hem, sleeves drawn back from her wrists. A loose braid had been pinned up, though several strands had escaped and moved against her neck in the breeze. There was a streak of paint along one finger.

He stopped before he meant to.

He had imagined her too often and too badly. Younger than she was. Colder than she would be. Kinder than he deserved. The woman before him belonged to none of those failures.

She turned before he spoke.

For a moment neither of them moved.

Her eyes found him and stayed there. He saw recognition strike and settle. Not shock. Not welcome. Not refusal. Something more difficult: the past arriving at her gate in travel-stained boots.

Riven bowed his head slightly.

“Lyriel.”

She set the brush down with care, as though her hand had every right to its steadiness.

“Riven.”
His name in her voice nearly undid him. She had not sharpened it. She had not softened it either.

“You found the house,” she said.

It was so ordinary that he almost had no answer.

“Thaelirian gave me directions.”

A faint movement touched her mouth. “Of course he did.”

The fountain spoke behind them. A bird shifted in the branches overhead and sent down two white petals.

Riven took one step closer. No more.

“I hope I’m not intruding.”

Lyriel looked at him with the clear attention he remembered from long ago: the look she gave to unfinished work, to injured creatures, to foolish young captains.

“You are,” she said.

He accepted that.

Then she added, “But not unwelcome.”

Something in him eased and ached at once.

“I wasn’t sure.”

“No,” she said. “I imagine not.”

Her eyes moved over him then: the cloak, the worn boots, the lines at his eyes, the bow at his back, the stillness where there had once been restlessness. He did not look away.

“You have changed.”

“Yes.”

“So have you,” he said.

This time the movement at her mouth became a small, sad smile.

“That was going to happen whether you returned to observe it or not.”

The words were gentle. They struck cleanly.

“I know.”

That seemed to matter. Not enough to soften her, exactly. Enough that she did not have to brace against him.

She gestured to a bench beneath the nearest tree. “Will you sit?”

He did.

She sat across from him, not beside him.

Up close, he noticed the blue paint near her thumb again, the tiny crease between her brows, the place where she had wiped her wrist on her sleeve without looking.

“I heard of your tournament,” she said. “And the war after it. News travels, even when people do not.”
“I wrote to you.”

“I know.”

He looked up.

“I received the letter,” she said.

For a heartbeat he could only sit with that.

“I waited for you in the stands,” he said.

Her face changed in a small way. Her gaze dropped to her hands, then returned to him.

“I heard that too.”

He had thought of that waiting as private. Foolishly, perhaps. There had been a lonely dignity in it when it belonged only to him. Now, set before her, it looked smaller and more human.

“Why didn’t you come?” he asked.

There was no accusation in it. He was grateful for that much.

Lyriel looked toward the canvas.

“Because I did not know whom you had invited.” She turned back to him. “Me, or the elf maiden who loved you before you left. Someone you wished to see, or someone you needed to prove yourself to.”

Riven sat still.

“I could not travel all that way,” she said, “to discover your letter had been written to a memory wearing my name.”

No defense came. None would have survived the air between them.

“I think,” he said slowly, “if you had asked me then, I would have said that was not true.”

“Yes,” she said. “I think you would have.”

He met her eyes.

“And I might have been wrong.”

She gave the smallest nod.

The breeze moved through the leaves above them. A bee worked at a bloom near the wall, patient and loud in the pause.

“I did not come here,” Riven said, “to ask you to forget what I did.”

Her attention gathered on him.
“I left,” he said. “For a long time I told myself it was honor. I had failed. I had been stripped of command. I had no right to remain.”

He drew a breath.

“That was true enough to hide behind.”

Lyriel did not move.

“I was ashamed,” he said. “And I could not bear for you to see me ashamed and love me anyway. I told myself I was sparing you the ruin of me. I was not. I chose distance because I did not know how to stay without first becoming worthy of staying.”

The fountain ran on. Somewhere beyond the wall, a gate closed softly.

Lyriel’s fingers folded together in her lap.

“No,” she said. “You did not.”

There was no triumph in it. That was what made it hard to hear.
