The candles had burned to nubs by the time the last petitioner left.

Astor sat behind the councilor's desk and pressed hands against his eyes, trying to ignore the pain that had started earlier in the back of his neck was creeping up the base of his spine. 

The dispute had been a land claim: two families, a shared creek, a fence post that may or may not have been moved three feet east sometime since the start of the Pitax War. Each side had brought witnesses. Each side had brought documents. One woman had brought her elderly mother, who had wept.
He'd ruled in neither's favor, precisely, and in both's favor, approximately. A surveyor would be sent after wartime. The creek would be treated as common water. The fence would stay where it was until the surveyor arrived, and anyone who touched it before then would answer to the Crown. It was the sort of judgment that left everyone mildly unhappy, which he had learned years ago was often the mark of a fair one.

He opened the desk's drawer to pull out a shot glass.  In his younger days, it would've been a tankard, emblazoned with Cayden's grinning face.  When he was younger, he'd much modeled his life after the Accidental God.  Living large, gripping the world fiercely with both hands as he went along for the ride.

He was 42 now, and he had learned that sometimes the world needed to be held very carefully, with open palms.

He pulled out his hot water flask and a packet of carefully dried herbs.  They mixed in the shot glass until the liquid turned a light, cool green.

She had rejected his invitation to the wedding, and when he'd brought his first child around, she threatened to eat her.  He brought the second child over anyway.
