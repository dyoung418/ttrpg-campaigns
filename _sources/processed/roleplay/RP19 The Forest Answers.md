    Sunlight spilled through pines and broad oaks, slicing the road into bars of gold and shadow. The air smelled of resin and crushed needles, wildflowers sweetening the breeze. Birds sang above, indifferent to the wagons groaning beneath their cargo of grain, salted meat, and barrels stamped with Irovetti’s golden goblet. The guards trudged with the dull rhythm of men bound by coin or lash, shields knocking against dented mail. None looked up. None noticed how still the trees had become. “Silverstep,” one muttered, shifting his mace. “Soft city, softer people. We’ll drink their cellars dry.” His companion gave a thin smile, yellow teeth showing. “Drink, aye. And when the gates fall, we’ll have our pick of scholars and scribes. Fine hands for holding quills — or other things.” Their laughter was coarse and mean, loud enough to scatter a jay from the branches above. The first arrow cut it short. It punched through the smiling man’s chest, snapping him backward into the dust. Another hissed past and buried in a throat. Then the forest erupted. Arrows screamed in from every side. Shields jerked up, too slow. Wagon covers caught fire in sudden blooms, tar and pitch spitting black smoke. Horses reared, chains snapping. Cloaked figures surged out of the green undergrowth, blades low and swift. No cry, no warning — only the crack of splitting wood, the hiss of burning sap, and the relentless thrum of bowstrings.
    
2. The mercenaries fought. Swords hacked wild, shields slammed forward, maces swung in desperate arcs. But it was no line, only men struggling alone. A shieldman blocked two arrows before the third slipped a gap and dropped him to the mud. A sellsword parried three strikes in quick succession before a curved elven blade slid under his guard and opened his chest. Others cursed, prayed, or ran, their panic loud against the forest’s silence. Riven moved through it all without haste. His Feywind Bow burned in his hands, fire and astral light curling with each shot. Every arrow loosed like a breath: through chain, through leather, through flesh. He gave no shout, no curse, no wasted word. A mercenary lunged, sword flashing. Riven drew and loosed in the same motion, the shaft burying through the man’s eye. He stepped past without pause, as though nothing more than brush had blocked his path. Minutes later, it was done. Smoke drifted from burning wagons. The last groans faded into the crackle of fire. Birds began to call again, tentative but steady. The Rangers stood among the wreckage with easy discipline, their banter already returning. One held up a helm with his arrow still lodged through it, laughing that he’d keep it as a trophy. Another shoved a surrendered sellsword toward the road and said he’d make a fine mule for the centaurs. Riven walked among them calm as ever, men nodding as he passed. He stopped before a captured captain, crouched, and held the man’s gaze. “Where were these wagons bound?” The captain spat. His fellow sneered. “Ask all you like. Pitax will grind your villages to ash.” Riven didn’t answer. He turned instead to a thin driver kneeling apart, his eyes wide with fear. “You know,” Riven said, voice steady. The man swallowed. His hands shook. “The… the Golden Talons. Two battalions. They march. For Silverstep.”
    
3. Riven considered him a long moment, then nodded. “Double rations for this one. The rest, east, to the centaurs.” Nearby, a wounded mercenary slumped against a broken wheel, blood soaking his tunic. His curses had dwindled to groans. Riven knelt, pressed a hand to the wound, and tipped a flask to his lips. “Drink.” The man obeyed, too weak to resist. Riven rose and moved on. Mercy for the broken, arrows for the defiant. When the last wagon collapsed into embers, a scarred veteran fell in beside him, cleaning his short sword with a strip of cloth. Tovan’s voice was rough with fatigue, dry with humor. “We could rest here, lord. Let the men eat, laugh a bit. They’ve been marching since yesterday.” Riven’s gaze stayed on the dark wall of trees ahead. His bow was still warm in his hand. “The Golden Talons march for Cassandra. They outnumber her two to one. We must reach her before they do.” Tovan snorted, shaking his head but matching Riven’s pace. “Always the hard road.” The Rangers slipped back into the forest, their passage leaving no broken fern, no snapped branch only smoke and ash fading in their wake.

    The rhythm was wrong. That was the first thing Almon d’Avoine noticed. Not the shift in the dappled sunlight lighting up the canvas of the supply wagon, but the cadence. The monotonous groan of the wagons, the rhythmic tramp of the guards plodding alongside—it was a dull, predictable beat. But the subtle beats of the environment _outside_ their plodding had changed. The realization came quickly -- the rhythmic accompaniment of birdsong that had accented the woods was gone. He was moving before the first scream. The coarse laughter of the mercenaries outside had been a grating annoyance; its sudden end was confirmation. He didn’t reach for a sword. A sword was useless against an enemy you couldn’t see. Instead, his gloved hands found the seams of the false floorboard. He had installed it himself.
    
7. The first arrow targeting his wagon struck with a wet punch, the sound tearing through the air. The wagon shuddered as the driver was torn from his seat. Almon was already gone, slipping into the grime and dust beneath the chassis, his fingers finding purchase on the thick axle support. He kept his breathing steady, the world reduced to thudding impacts against the wood above and the screams of men and horses. An arrow hissed past his hiding spot, followed by the heavy fall of a guard. The man landed less than a yard away, a feathered shaft buried in his throat. An opportunity. From the angle of the shaft and the others now pincushioning the wagon’s side, he calculated the primary lines of fire.They were disciplined, firing in volleys from multiple positions. Fools fought, but clever men hid. Almon intended to be very clever.
    
8. The wagon cover erupted in a sudden bloom of fire, spitting tar and smoke. It was the perfect cover. As black smoke billowed down, he scurried from beneath his shelter. In one swift motion, he was on the dead guard. The man’s cloak came free with a jerk. His dented helmet was still warm. Another arrow lay on the ground nearby, its shaft splintered. Almon snapped the arrow shaft further, not quite in two, leaving a sharp, angled break. He slid the helmet on, then wedged the arrowhead into the eye-slit, twisting the shaft so the broken wood pressed harmlessly against his cheekbone. To any casual glance, it was a killing shot through the eye. He collapsed back into the mud beside the burning wheel, letting his limbs go slack. He slowed his breathing. The battle was already over; it was a slaughter, not a fight. He listened to the sounds of the victors.Their banter was relaxed, professional. One laughed about a helmet he’d claimed as a trophy.
    
9. Then came a calm, steady voice that cut through the rest. Almon tracked it as it moved to the captured captain. “Where were these wagons bound?” The captain’s answer was spit and defiance about Pitax grinding villages to ash. A fool’s response. The calm voice moved on, addressing a different captive—a driver, by the sound of his trembling fear... “You know,” the voice stated, not asked. The broken reply came a moment later. “The… the Golden Talons. Two battalions. They march. For Silverstep.”
    
10. Almon’s mind seized on the disclosure. They knew. “The Golden Talons march for Cassandra,” the leader said to one of his own men a moment later. “They outnumber her two to one. We must reach her before they do.” The intelligence was a brand in his mind. _Cassandra_. That was his target. He remained motionless as the Rangers stripped the wagons and dealt with the prisoners. He heard them fade back into the trees, leaving no sound of passage. For a long count, he lay still, the smell of smoke and blood thick in the air. When the only sounds were the crackle of the dying fires and the tentative return of birdsong, he moved. Almon d’Avoine pushed himself up, pulling the arrow from his helmet. His face was smeared with soot and dirt, a perfect disguise. He knew what he needed to accomplish now, and the pieces of a plan were already sliding into place in his mind.
