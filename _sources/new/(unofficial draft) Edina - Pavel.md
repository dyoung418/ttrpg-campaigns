  The inner courtyard of the Old Fortress at Nightmarch is busy in the way of things that precede terrible events — purposeful, quiet, no  wasted motion. Provisions are being checked and loaded. Two rangers near the eastern wall are testing bowstrings without speaking. A
  group by the gate is leaning over a map, their voices too low to carry. Three days ago, Naryssa's forces hit a settlement to the south.  What the scouts found there, people repeat only once and then stop.

  Riven is inside with the council. Lucas is at his prayers somewhere. The morning sky is the pale, uncommitted gray of early light that  hasn't decided what it will become.

  In the far corner of the courtyard, where the old wall meets the stable block, a large black panther lies curled with her tail across  her nose. Pavel is a few feet away, checking the lashings on his pack — each one, in order, with the focused economy of a man who has  done this ten thousand times and does it again anyway because that is the kind of man he is.

  Edina Aldori has been watching from the corridor archway for longer than she planned. She had told herself she would approach him when she was ready. She is beginning to understand that ready is not somewhere she is going to arrive on her own.

  She steps out of the archway. The boots she is wearing today are new.

  2. ### Edina — [continued]

  She crosses the courtyard at a walking pace, on a direct line. Not cautious, not stealthy. She knows better than to approach this  particular man the way one approaches a skittish animal — he would read that immediately, and he would resent it, and he would be right  to. She has thought carefully about how to do this. Directness is not always kindness, but with Pavel, she suspects, it comes closer  than most alternatives.

  She stops a few feet away. Not too far — that would be a retreat. Not so close it feels like a maneuver. The panther opens one amber  eye, considers her, and closes it again.

  "Pavel." Just his name. No other preamble. Enough to make clear that she came specifically to find him, and that she found what she was  looking for.

  She holds his gaze steadily. Her eyes are gray, and they give very little back.

  "I know you've been hoping to get out of this courtyard before anyone talked to you." A dry note in it, not unkind — just honest. The  acknowledgment of something real. "I won't keep you long."

  She doesn't look at his pack, or his weapons, or the gate. She looks at him.

  "I owe you an apology. For the feast." One beat. "I should have done more to put you at ease.  Those were old boots, actually. I'd been meaning to replace them for months."

  3. ###  [Pavel]
  
  Pavel's hands go still on the lashing.

  He'd heard her coming — different footfall from the soldiers, new sole leather, deliberate direction — and had conducted a brief,  internal argument with himself about whether he could plausibly be too occupied to notice. He'd concluded, with the resignation of a  man accepting an unavoidable situation, that this would not work on Edina Aldori.

  He's been bracing for this conversation, or some version of it, since the feast. He has run through the possible versions multiple  times. In none of them did he come out well. He had catalogued his failures from that evening with the systematic thoroughness he  applies to everything — there were sixteen if you counted generously, eleven if you were strict. The boots ranked fourth. Above the  bench, above the language, below the vomiting itself and the part where he'd apparently been ready to assault one of his kingdom's  political allies over a dance.

  He has been over it enough times that he should be past it.

  He is not past it.

  "They weren't," he says. His voice comes out flat, which is not quite what he intended. "They were new. The stitching was—" He stops.  He is aware this is the wrong thing to be focusing on. He looks at her new boots. Confirms his assessment. Looks away.

  "I'm sorry," he says. "For the boots. And the rest of it." A pause. "I was not at my best."

  He is aware this is an exceptional understatement. Cat, behind him, shifts without lifting her head.

  ---
  4. ### Edina

  She hadn't expected him to notice the stitching.

  Something almost happens to her expression. She lets it happen just enough — not amusement at his expense, which she wouldn't want him  to read, but the kind of involuntary response that comes when someone surprises you in a way you find, unexpectedly, worth being
  surprised by. She lets it pass.

  "I know you weren't," she says. "I should have come to find you after. I didn't." She says this without apology or performance — just  as a fact she is naming. She has found that people who cannot easily read social signals are often far more comfortable with plain  acknowledgment than with softened approaches that require them to decode layers. It removes the part where you have to guess what  someone is actually saying. "That was a choice I made. I could have made a different one."

  She glances at the panther, who is watching both of them with an amber patience that suggests she has formed views on this  conversation.

  She brings her eyes back.

  "I know what you did in the competition," she says. The dry edge leaves her voice entirely. "All of you. You were opponents. You had no  obligation to stop. Every moment you spent fighting that creature was points you weren't earning, and you knew that." Her voice stays  level, but it carries the weight of something she has thought about far more than she shows. "I've thought about that a great deal, in  the time since."

  She meets his eyes.

  "I'm glad it was you, Pavel. I'm glad you were there — not because it would have changed what happened to my sister." She does not look  away from him. "It wouldn't have. I know that. But I was not alone when she died, and that mattered. And I have never forgotten whose  face I saw when I looked up from the ground." A pause. "You don't forget the person who makes sure you're not alone in a moment like  that."

  ---
  5. ###  [Pavel]

  He is very still.

  Not the fidgeting stillness of a man suppressing a response — the particular motionlessness of complete attention. Cat lifts her head  from the crook of her tail, slowly, without urgency, and pads across the courtyard stone to sit beside him. Her shoulder presses  against the outside of his knee. He puts his hand on her head without looking.

  He is grateful for this, in a way he could not easily articulate.

  He has, in the course of this conversation, had to revise his understanding of several things he thought he knew. The revision process  takes time he does not currently have. He is aware that she is waiting. He is also aware that the version of himself that deflects,  that minimizes, that says I didn't do much, the others were the real— is the version that will keep this morning exactly as far from  him as all the other mornings have been. He decides, with a clarity that surprises him slightly, that he does not want that.

  "I remember it," he says. His voice is rough, which he ascribes to the early air. "All of it." A longer pause. He pushes past the  deflection he can feel forming, the one about how it was just what any of them would have done, which is not even true. "I'm glad you  weren't alone too."

  It comes out more simply than he intended. He nearly walks it back.

  He doesn't.

  ---
  6. ### Edina
  
  She had come prepared for him to retreat. She has spent considerable thought on the geometry of this conversation — where the exits  were, what form the deflections would take. She had three approaches ready to meet three different kinds. Pavel is a man who has more  exits than most, and he uses them with the unconscious efficiency of a creature whose survival has depended on knowing where the ways  out are.

  He didn't use any of them.

  She had not specifically planned for that.

  She looks at him for a moment — this quiet man who grew up sleeping next to dogs and understood animals before he understood people,  who stalked across a feasting hall drunk and furious and fell to his knees when she said his name, who stood at the edge of her street
  in Restov and—

  She stops that thought. Now is not the time.

  "We're leaving soon," she says. "And I don't know what you're walking into." A pause. Not theatrical — just the gap where in case  lives. She lets him hear it.

  "I've been thinking about what to say since before dawn. I kept reaching for the careful version — the version that gives you room to  not answer, that leaves us both somewhere comfortable." She glances, briefly, toward the corridor where people are moving with purpose.
  "It reminded me of the Sword and the Suitor." A dry note, but not a light one. "It's a good dance. I've danced the Lady more times  than I can count. But I've never been comfortable with the way it works — keeping the man guessing, making him read your mind or take a  hit from the sword." She meets his eyes directly. "That's not how I want a real thing to be. "

  She holds herself still.

  "I think about you, Pavel. More than I have any ordinary reason to. I have for a long time." She says it plainly, the way she'd state a  fact she'd verified. "You deserve to know that before you walk out that gate. You deserve to know that you are in someone's thoughts  in a way that is not ordinary — in a way that has been true since long before you vomited on my boots."

  The faintest thing happens at the corner of her mouth. She lets that one through.

  "And I'm not willing to let you leave here thinking you were never in anyone's class."


  ---
  7. ###  [Pavel]

  He is quiet.

  Long enough that someone who didn't know him might worry. Cat looks up at him. The amber eyes ask something. He doesn't have an answer  for Cat.

  He has spent — he does a rough accounting — a significant fraction of the last several years constructing an accurate picture of where  he stands in the world. The picture includes: the kennel, his mother's reputation, the class Edina Aldori comes from and the class he  doesn't, the feast, the boots, the bench. He has returned to this picture many times. He has considered it from different angles. He  believes it to be an honest picture.

  It turns out some of his measurements were wrong.

  He doesn't know yet what to do with that. He won't know for a while. He is not a man who revises quickly, and he is not sure this is  something that gets revised so much as just — lived with differently.

  He looks at Cat. Cat tells him nothing useful.

  He looks at Edina.

  "I went looking for you," he says. His voice is even, which costs him something. "In Restov. I did that several times." He has never  said this to anyone. He has barely acknowledged it to himself. It seems suddenly dishonest not to say it — she has said something real,  and meeting it with nothing is a kind of lie by omission that he finds he doesn't want to commit. "I didn't come to the door," he  adds. "Because I thought—" He stops. Tries a different way in. "I've been certain, for a long time, that I wasn't what — that you  wouldn't—"

  The thought refuses to assemble itself correctly. He gives up on the ending.

  "I think about you too," he says instead. To the ground. "I have for a long time."

  Cat makes a sound. It is not her warning sound, not her rebuke sound. It is something smaller — the sound she makes when she settles  somewhere she trusts. Pavel does not look at her.

  ---
  8. ### Edina

  I went looking for you.

  She hears it. She holds it.

  Several times.

  She thinks of the girl from the inn — "a quiet man, dark hair, he keeps finding reasons to walk past the estate, I've seen him three  times this month" — and the part where she'd thought: he'll come to the door eventually. And then he hadn't, and she'd eventually  understood that he wasn't going to, and she'd thought: then I'll have to.

  She is glad she did.

  She steps forward, one step — just enough to close the distance from conversation to something else. Not quite close enough to touch.  Just close enough that neither of them can pretend this is incidental. She stands there, at the edge of his space, and looks at him  directly: this man who learned to read animals before he learned to read people and is perhaps, she suspects, better at both than he  gives himself credit for.

  "If you come back," she says, quietly, "come to the door."

  Not when. She is too honest for when. But she means it completely — the invitation and the weight of it both — and she wants him to  understand that.

  From inside the keep, Riven's voice carries into the courtyard. Low, calm, the particular cadence he uses when he is telling people  what they are about to go do together. The morning has run its course.

  She holds his gaze one moment longer.

  "For what it's worth," she says — and she means this too, every word of it — "I always thought you were exactly in my class. I just  wasn't sure you knew it."

  She turns and walks back toward the archway. She does not look back. She doesn't need to. She knows he's watching. She has known since  the feast that he watches, from corners, from elevated positions, from the edge of things — this man who learned early that the edge is
  where you survive.

  She hopes, walking through the archway and back into the stone corridor, that the edge is where he survives today too.

  9. ###  [Pavel]

  He watches her go.

  Cat watches too. Her tail moves, once, twice — the slow arc that Pavel has learned to read as the intersection of interesting and  something that is probably the panther equivalent of I told you so. He looks down at her. Cat looks back up at him with the unbothered  authority of a creature who has never doubted her own judgment.

  "Don't," he says.

  Cat yawns. Her canine teeth are approximately the length of his finger. She has never been remotely intimidated by him and she is not  starting now.

  He looks back at the archway. She is gone. From the keep, Riven's voice continues — he's giving assignments now, Pavel can tell by the  rhythm of it. There is work to do. There is a gate to walk out of. There are people somewhere to the south who need what he's about to  go provide.

  He picks up his pack. He checks the lashing. It's fine. It was fine before. He knows it's fine.

  Come to the door.

  He will. He decides this with the same uncomplicated certainty with which he decides things about Cat, about the pack, about where to  hold a line. He will walk out the gate, and he will do what the day asks of him, and then he will come back, and he will come to the  door. That is settled.

  He is not sure he believes the last part she said — the part about his class. He has believed the opposite for a long time, and belief  does not revise overnight. But she is not a woman who says things she doesn't mean. He knows this much about her. She says what she  says, and she means it.

  He might believe it by the time he comes to the door.

  He shoulders the pack.

  "Alright," he says to Cat.

  Cat rises. She is twelve stone of panther and she moves like water and she has walked at his left heel since the night she came out of  the dark and sat beside him without being asked, without reason, without anything but the simple animal decision that this was where  she would be.

  He heads for the gate.
