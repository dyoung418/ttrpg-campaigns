    # **The Empty Goblet**
    
    The camp of the Golden Talons lay in a meadow in lines exact as a ledger. Blue-dyed tents, every shield boss stamped with the golden goblet of Pitax, fires measured out in rows. Men diced by lamplight, oiled their mail, muttered about the supply wagons that still hadn’t come. These were veterans of the River Kingdoms, men who had sold their spears to kings and bandit lords alike. Short rations were as familiar as steel. Lord Darian heard them but gave no answer. To him, their voices were the baying of hounds, nothing more. He sat beneath his pavilion, boots on the campaign table, white plume helm laid neatly beside him. His map of Silverstep was weighted with stones — a city of towers, temples, and halls of learning drawn in tidy lines. He studied it not as a commander studies terrain, but as a man considers an estate about to fall into his hands. “She was a thief,” Darian said, wine dark in his goblet. “A bandit in the service of the Stag Lord. That is the measure of their captain Cassandra. Freehaven places its jewels in the keeping of cutpurses. This is who we fight.” His captains chuckled — sharp-eyed sellswords who had fought for Mivon, for the Thorn, for half a dozen crowns before Irovetti’s. One, scarred and pipe-stem clenched between his teeth, only shrugged. “She held the ditch today longer than expected. Cost us men.” Darian’s smile did not reach his eyes. “So would a wolf in a trap.” He raised his goblet. “Hear me now. I will not drink again until it is Silverstep’s wine, poured into this cup — Irovetti’s gift. And I will drink it in the city’s hall.” They raised cups, some with zeal, some with weary cynicism. Darian drained his last swallow and set the goblet down with deliberate care. It stood empty beside his map, catching the firelight.
    
2. At dawn, the Golden Talons dressed ranks. Shields locked, spears gleamed, drums rolled steady as a forge-hammer. Darian mounted his horse — black, scarred, its breath pluming in the cold air. He rode the line in silence, men striking spear to shield as he passed. Beyond the meadows, the pale towers of Silverstep shimmered faint through the haze. Darian did not shout. He did not need to. He touched the empty goblet at his belt and smiled.
    
3. # **Council in the Dark**
    
    The Guard lay quiet beneath the pines, armor stacked beside them, halberds in rows that caught the faint ember glow. Most men lay under their cloaks, awake but unmoving. A few scraped whetstones down blades, the sound thin as insects. One whispered his wife’s name until another hissed at him to stop. The air smelled of resin, iron, and old blood. Discipline kept them still; unease kept them awake. The Rangers slipped in without warning. One moment the treeline stood empty, the next shadows threaded among the sleeping Guard. Men jerked upright, fists tightening on halberds, mutters rising sharp with fear. Then a lantern caught the faint runes gleaming along a bow, warm light like fire. A voice whispered, “Riven.” The camp eased, not with cheer but with the sound of breath let out through teeth, of shoulders lowering. A sergeant muttered, “Thirty.” Relief, yes, but it changed little. Cassandra stepped forward, helm under her arm, braid stiff with sweat and blood. “We thought you’d gone north,” she said. Riven’s eyes moved across the camp, men staring, the shrouded dead by the fire, before settling on her. “I sent Ekundayo with the other rangers north to reinforce Serenko.” “And you,” he continued, “you held.” They gathered in a grove apart, a plank set on stumps between them, charcoal scratched into river, meadow, valley. Stones marked ridges and ford. A lantern hissed, throwing light that sharpened every line of fatigue in their faces. Two sergeants stood with Cassandra, Hadrik, broad and blunt, and Senn, lean, his voice always tight with caution. “They’ll march at dawn,” Cassandra said. “Two hundred at least. My Guard are a hundred still standing. Add your thirty Rangers, that’s a hundred and thirty against over two hundred.” Senn said, “That’s suicide.”
    
4. Hadrik spat into the dirt. “Then the streets. Fight house to house. Make them bleed for every step.” Senn shook his head. “Half our men are bloodied already. They won’t hold two clashes. We’ll be run down before dusk.” Cassandra’s jaw tightened. “And we’d lose Silverstep before nightfall.” She leaned on the plank, her hand smearing charcoal. “I saw their captain. Darian. He rides as if the ground already belongs to him. He doesn’t see us — only through us. To him, we’re rabble in stolen armor. He’ll drive hard the moment he thinks the line bends.” Riven’s gaze met hers, steady. He said nothing, but the silence pressed her to continue. “I know the look,” she said. “I wore it once. Steel in my hands, sure the world couldn’t touch me. It blinds you. If we show him weakness, he’ll come too far, too fast.” Riven moved a stone from the meadow into the valley. “Then we give him what he wants. Forty halberds in the meadow, dressed to look strong but already bending. When they give ground, they do it slow, in order, across the ford. He’ll think he has you broken.” He placed two stones on the ridges. “My Rangers here, waiting. Thirty arrows at the right moment are enough. We kill their officers, sow fire in the ranks, break their wall from above. Mercenaries don’t die for pride. They die when their paymaster falls and their captains lie in the dirt.” Hadrik scowled. “And if the Guard break before we reach the ford?” “They won’t,” Riven said. Not loud, not forceful — certain. Senn’s mouth worked as if to argue, but no words came. Cassandra looked at her men, thought of their faces in the dark, the weight of their halberds, the steadiness they still showed though their bodies bled. There was no safe choice left — only this. She slid the last stone into the valley’s throat. “Then we gamble the city on Willowshade.” Riven gave a single nod. For him, it was decided.
    
5. The lantern sputtered, its flame collapsing low. Outside, the Guard lay silent under the trees. In the east, the first gray light touched Silverstep’s towers, pale and indifferent to the cost to come.
    
6. # **Willowshade**
    
    Mist lay heavy in the meadow, muffling the Guard’s breath where they stood with halberds sunk into churned earth. Their armor was dented, some straps held with cord, but the line was straight. Cassandra walked before them with helm under her arm, braid damp against her shoulder. “Close ranks,” she said. “Step when I step.” Across the ford, the Golden Talons formed in their hundreds, shields locking rim to rim beneath banners of blue and gold. Drums carried steady. Darian rode behind them, plume bright, sword lifted as though pointing out a prize already won. The drums quickened. The Talons advanced. The lines met with a crash of wood and iron. Halberds hooked shield rims, dragging men sideways; spearpoints darted into gaps, forcing grunts and curses. Cassandra wrenched her blade, felt the crack of bone through the haft before her man fell. The Guard bent but filled every gap, boots gouging deep furrows in wet ground. “Hold!” she shouted, voice raw. Still the wall pressed forward. Darian’s voice rose over the drums: “Silverstep is ours! Drive them!” His plume wheeled behind the shield wall, urging them on. Step by bloody step the Guard gave ground, retreating into the ford. Cold water pulled at their knees as they hauled the wounded with them. The current swirled cloudy with mud and darker stains. Cassandra’s cadence carried steady: “Step. Hold. Step.” Beyond the ford the hollow narrowed, ridges closing in with dark pines. The Guard wheeled, still tight, drawing the Talons deeper. Darian drove them harder, mistaking retreat for collapse. “On them! Into the valley!” The trap closed.
    
7. Arrows hissed down from both ridges, officers dropping mid-command, drummers sprawling across their skins. Roots tore from the banks, tangling legs, ripping shields wide. Then the storm struck: hail hammered helms, sleet slicked the earth to muck. On the ridge, Riven loosed arrow after arrow, each shaft streaked with fire and pale light. His Rangers fired in rhythm, each volley cutting the Talons’ order to ribbons. The blizzard came next, wind shrieking through the hollow. Banners tore, men slammed into each other blind, shields wrenched from numb fingers. Discipline buckled in the gale. “Wheel!” Cassandra cried. Her Guard pivoted, halberds braced across the valley’s throat. The first fugitives ran onto their points and were shoved back into the chaos. She lifted her weapon to steady the line. A spear slid under her arm and burst through her ribs. Air hissed from her chest. She drove her halberd home anyway, hooking a shield rim and dragging it down so Halvik beside her could thrust. Her knees buckled at last, the haft slipping from her grip. The sergeant dragged her back. Darian rose in his stirrups, sword lifted. “With me! Hold—” An arrow struck him through the breastplate, flaming as it passed through. He folded soundlessly and slid from the saddle, plume crushed in the mud. The goblet-banner sagged. The Talons broke. Some flung down shields and begged for quarter; others ran and were cut down by halberds or Rangers’ shafts from the ridges. Two hundred men dissolved into fugitives, their discipline gone to storm and blood. The Guard did not lower their weapons until the valley was still.
    
8. Cassandra lay against her sergeant’s arm, breath rattling, eyes fixed on the ford. Riven crouched beside her, hand pressed to the wound, whispering the words of a spell. Green light flickered, guttered, died in the tide of blood. He tried again, jaw set, and watched it vanish. Cassandra’s eyes locked on his. “Don’t,” she rasped. He let the magic fade, tightened the strap at her shoulder easing her pain. “You set the trap and won the day,” he said. Her lips twitched faintly. “Then… don’t waste it.” Her hand slackened from the haft. Her gaze dulled. The Guard stood silent, armor cracked, faces gray with fatigue, halberds rooted in churned ground. Riven checked the strap of her cuirass one last time, squared the halberd across her chest, and stepped back. “This is the price,” he said quietly. Far off, the towers of Silverstep caught the sun. In the hollow below, smoke drifted low across the bodies.
    
9. # **Ash on the Lake**
    
    The march back was broken and halting. Men limped, some with arms in slings, others leaning on staves cut from roadside branches. One Guard carried another’s helm because the weight was too much. Cassandra’s body lay high on a bier of crossed spears, her braid dark against battered steel. The column made no sound but for boots in mud and the rasp of leather. Silverstep crowded the lanes. Relief flickered across faces — then stilled when they saw the bier. The hush that followed was sharper than any cry. A woman shoved through and caught her son, half-lifting him despite the limp in his leg. A baker stood in the street with flour caked to his arms, dough drying in the bowl on the cart behind him. A scribe dropped his scrolls and let them scatter across the stones, not stooping to gather them. At the steps of Desna’s temple the Guard set her down. They laid her halberd across her chest, braid straightened along the armor. One by one, the soldiers leaned their weapons against the wall and stood bareheaded beside them, the silence holding. Riven faced the Guard, then the city gathered behind. His words were blunt, not raised for ceremony. “She spent her life on this field,” he said. “We owe it to her and the others who fell today to live true and fight on.” That was all. He checked the halberd, squared it so it lay neat, and turned away before the crowd pressed close. His shoulders were rigid, his eyes fixed not on the bier but on the pale sweep of lake and mountains beyond. That night, no torches burned in the city. A boat slid out onto the lake with Cassandra’s body. The fire took slow hold, smoke trailing low across the water. On the shore the Guard stood in silence, the city behind them. The only sounds were the crack of burning wood and the lap of water against the hull.
