    The smoke rose first, dark coils on the horizon where the farmsteads burned. Cassandra stood at the meadow’s edge, braid falling over her shoulder, halberd heavy in her hand. Behind her, the white towers of Silverstep shimmered in the afternoon sun, the bells of Desna echoing faintly across the lake. In front of her, only the hedgerow, the ditch, and the enemy’s drums. She had twenty of the Silverstep Guard with her assessing the strength of the enemy, halberdiers she had drilled until their arms ached, men she knew by name. Halvik, whose voice could bark cadence through a gale. Joren, the mason’s apprentice, steady as stone. Luko, a fisherman’s boy, eyes flicking back toward the water as if he longed for his net instead of a steel shaft. They braced along the ditch, armor bright but dented, halberds leveled. Across the meadow came the vanguard of Pitax. Blue banners with the golden goblet rippled above a column of sixty mercenaries, shields high, spears forward, armor polished bright. These were no rabble. They marched with precision, boots striking the ground in time with the drums. At their head, Lord Darian rode his horse at a walk, white plume tossing in the wind. He surveyed the Guard as though they were an inconvenience on his road. “Archers, loose!” Cassandra called. A thin volley hissed out, five bowmen loosing together. A handful of mercenaries staggered, one dropped. The rest raised shields and kept coming. “Back two! Brace!” The archers slipped behind the hedge. The halberds lowered, hooks ready. The clash was sudden and brutal, spears punching across the ditch, halberds hooking and dragging, shields smashing forward. Cassandra heard the wet sound of steel in flesh, the grunt of her men straining against superior weight. Halvik roared cadence: “Thrust, hook, heave!” and the Guard obeyed, dragging two mercenaries into the ditch where they were finished with blades.
    
2. But numbers told. Sixty against twenty. The line bent, men grunted as shields slammed into their chests, a halberdier screamed as a spear tore into his belly. Cassandra herself caught a spear on her haft, twisted, and wrenched it free before driving her blade under a rim of steel. Blood sprayed hot across her cheek. “Fall back by files!” she shouted. The Guard moved as drilled, two at a time retreating, others covering. Arrows hissed overhead to slow pursuit, but the mercenaries pressed hard, their captain holding them in tight formation. Cassandra glimpsed Darian’s white plume, saw him raise his sword, then rein in. He did not chase. He didn’t need to. He let her men bleed and fall back, contempt written in the calm way he held the field. And besides, the rest of his troops would be here in the next few days. By the time Cassandra pulled her detachment back to the rise, three of her men lay dead in the meadow and five more staggered with wounds. The mercenaries reformed calmly, shields still locked, and planted their banner in the churned grass. Darian sat his horse watching, a man already convinced Silverstep was his. Cassandra moved among the wounded, tearing cloth with her own hands, binding wounds, steadying voices. The Guard were weary, shaken, but not broken. She needed to regroup with the remainder of the Guard, but it still wouldn’t be enough to hold out. She looked over the meadow where her dead lay, then to the blue-and-gold banners massed in the fading light. Once she had followed the Stag Lord, certain her life would end nameless in some ditch. Freehaven had given her a chance she had never earned, and she bore that trust like armor. She would stand until the last, but she had fought long enough to read the truth in the numbers. They did not have men enough to stop Pitax’s weight.
