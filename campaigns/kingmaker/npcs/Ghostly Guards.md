# Ghostly Guards

> **The Ashamed Dead.** The spectral soldiers of [[Nyrissa]]'s **first kingdom** — the one the Eldest
> destroyed when they sent the Jabberwock against her. They died failing to protect a realm that was
> already doomed, and out of shame they have stood guard at her door ever since, defending the queen
> whose punishment damned them. **8 at the Gatehouse (K1)**; 12 more in the Servants' Wing (K15).

> [!note] This is a *choice* fight, not a damage race
> These are the campaign's **first Shame creatures** and the party's introduction to **Forgive Foe**
> (see [[House at the Edge of Time#The Forgive Foe Mechanic]]). The whole point of the beat is that the
> party **discovers they can free an enemy instead of killing it** — the seed of the [[Nyrissa]] choice.
> Design so that **killing is futile** (rejuvenation) rather than lethal, so mercy is the *discovered*
> solution, not the *forced* one. See "Running the Gate" below.

## Statblock — Ghostly Guard

**Creature 15** · Uncommon · Medium · **Undead, Incorporeal, Spirit** *(elf in life; many were Calistrians)*

**Perception** +25; darkvision
**Languages** Elven, Common
**Skills** Intimidation +25, Stealth +28, Warfare Lore +26

**Str** −5, **Dex** +7, **Con** +5, **Int** +2, **Wis** +6, **Cha** +6

**AC** 37; **Fort** +24, **Ref** +28, **Will** +27
**HP** 180, **negative healing**; **Immunities** death effects, disease, paralyzed, poison, unconscious; **Resistances** all damage 10 (except *force*, *ghost touch*, and positive/vitality); a guard takes **double** from vitality damage
**Speed** fly 25 ft (hover)

- **Incorporeal** — moves through creatures and objects; can't take manipulate actions unless the ability is magical.
- **Shame (elves & Calistria worshippers)** — a guard takes a **−2 circumstance penalty** to attack rolls and DCs against such creatures, and — the load-bearing flavor — **its Strikes can't reduce an elf or Calistrian below 1 HP.** It physically cannot bring itself to land the killing blow on its own kind. *(This makes [[Riven]] and [[Lee]] the safe front line, and the visible flinch is the party's first clue.)*
- **Bound Rejuvenation** — while [[Nyrissa]] lives **and** the guard remains **unforgiven**, a guard reduced to 0 HP is not destroyed: its form scatters and **re-forms at the gate at full HP at the start of its next turn.** Damage cannot permanently end it. **Only Forgive Foe (crit success) removes a guard for good.**
- **Spectral Spear** ⬍ (magical, finesse, reach 10) — attack **+27**, **3d8+9 void** damage, plus **Sorrow's Weight**.
- **Sorrow's Weight** (on a hit) — the target relives the guards' shame for an instant; **DC 34 Will** or **frightened 1** (crit fail frightened 2 + can't reduce its frightened value below 1 while adjacent to any guard). A mind-affecting grief effect, not a killer — the gate *wears you down*, it doesn't slaughter you.

> [!note] Freed guard (crit-success Forgive Foe)
> The guard's shame breaks. It lowers its spear, looks at whoever forgave it — often lingering on
> [[Riven]] or [[Lee]] — and **dissolves into a quiet, relieved light** (permanently gone). See the
> reward under "Running the Gate."

## Running the Gate (K1) — the First Mercy

**The setup:** eight guards stand in ghostlight behind the **portcullis** (Hardness 18, HP 72). They
raise spears as the party crosses the threshold — and, at the two elves, **they falter.** Lead with that
image before any dice: spears levelled at Riven and Lee, arms straining, *unable to close the distance.*

### The discovery flow (surface Forgive Foe without hand-holding)
1. **The flinch (free).** Narrate the Shame in the fiction from round one — guards who won't advance on
   their own kind, who strike as if apologizing. A perceptive player reads it.
2. **Recall Knowledge** (Religion or Society, DC ~34): these are *bound* spirits held by **shame**, not
   malice; force can't free them — *"they don't want to be here."*
3. **The futility beat.** The first guard the party drops **re-forms next round** (Bound Rejuvenation).
   Let that land: you cannot kill your way through this gate. *This* is what turns the table toward
   another approach.
4. **The first attempt.** [[Lucas]] (Diplomacy/social kit) is the natural first mover — **Forgive Foe,
   DC 39.** A success shows it has teeth (−2 to that guard); a **crit success frees one on-screen**, and
   it dissolves in relief. Proof of concept for the entire endgame.
5. **Momentum.** Freed guards don't rejuvenate; the "fight" visibly *shrinks* as the party forgives
   rather than as it kills. That contrast is the lesson.

### The reward: mercy opens the door
When the party frees the guards (or enough of them), **the freed dead raise the portcullis themselves**
and stand aside — the soldiers of a fallen kingdom letting living heroes pass where they never could
save their own. The party skips the Hardness-18 gate-breaking entirely. *Mercy is literally the faster
door.* A freed guard may also leave a **whisper** (the Session 104 "reward"): the House is watching; and
*"the one with many faces keeps the elf-woman."* (Seeds [[Session 104 - The First Mercy]] Secrets 4, 5, 10.)

### If they brute-force it anyway
Valid — but make sure they **saw** the option and chose the sword, so the [[Nyrissa]] choice later isn't
a surprise. Mechanically it's a slog by design: rejuvenation means they must either **break the portcullis
the hard way** while eight guards re-form and chip them with Sorrow's Weight, or realize mid-grind that
force isn't working. Either way the lesson lands: *some things in this House can't be killed, only freed.*

> [!note] Why this fixes the six-PC problem *differently*
> Unlike [[Tarlaxian]] and [[The Wriggling Man]], the gate **isn't balanced on damage at all** — six
> honed PCs can't "win" it by output, because output doesn't remove guards. The encounter is a **puzzle
> with a social key**, so the party's action-economy advantage is spent *discovering the answer*, not
> trivializing a boss. No stat mods needed.

> The party **discovered Forgive Foe on their own.** [[Milo]] and [[Pavel]] ID'd the guards as **undead**
> (not fey) tied to [[Nyrissa]]'s conquest; Danny confirmed they could be **released, not just killed.**
> The mercy tactic became the night's spine — [[Astor]] opened it (failed but lowered the DC), [[Milo]]
> landed the first **release** (a guard *"drifted away like smoke"*), [[Lee]] made *"it's not your fault"*
> a recurring refrain that stacked penalties, and [[Pavel]] had [[Briar|Briara]] invoke **Nyrissa's own
> authority** to dismiss them. [[Riven]]'s **ghost-touch Astral Rune** did real work from the air.
> The session ended **mid-fight**: four damaged guards inside the guardhouse, one bloodied and immobilized
> outside — several already softened with stacked forgiveness penalties. Resolution continues in Session 105.

## The Emotional Core

These are Nyrissa's **victims**, not her loyalists — the dead of the kingdom she failed, standing guard
out of a shame that mirrors her own. Forgiving them is the party's **first act of the campaign's thesis**:
*not every enemy in this House is evil; many are wounds.* And it rhymes hard with [[Riven]] (a soldier
who failed to save a village and let shame master him for decades) and lands on the two elves the guards
*cannot bring themselves to kill.* Play the freed guards' relief as the emotional payoff — and let it sit
with the party as they step through the gate that mercy opened.

## Forgive Foe — quick reference

**DC 39** (Diplomacy or Society), 2 actions. Crit success: guard **freed** (gone for good). Success: −2
to its attacks/DCs until end of your next turn. Crit fail: immune to your attempts for 24 hours. Full
mechanic and the House's other Shame creatures: [[House at the Edge of Time#The Forgive Foe Mechanic]].
